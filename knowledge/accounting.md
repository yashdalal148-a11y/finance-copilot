# Accounting

## Three Financial Statements

The three financial statements are the Income Statement, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow Statement. They are interconnected and together provide a complete picture of a company's financial health.

**Linkages:**
- Net Income from the Income Statement flows to Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet and is the starting point of the Cash Flow Statement
- Changes in Balance Sheet items (working capital, CapEx) appear on the Cash Flow Statement
- Ending Cash on the Cash Flow Statement ties to Cash on the Balance Sheet

## Income Statement

The Income Statement measures profitability over a period of time.

**Key line items (top to bottom):**
- Revenue (Net Sales)
- Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
- **Gross Profit** = Revenue - COGS
- Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D)
- **EBITDA** = Gross Profit - OpEx + D&A (or Revenue - COGS - OpEx + D&A)
- Depreciation & Amortization
- **EBIT (Operating Income)** = EBITDA - D&A
- Interest Expense
- **EBT (Pre-tax Income)** = EBIT - Interest
- Income Tax
- **Net Income** = EBT - Tax

**Key margins:**
- Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue
- EBITDA Margin = EBITDA / Revenue
- Operating Margin = EBIT / Revenue
- Net Margin = Net Income / Revenue

## Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet is a snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time.

**Fundamental equation:** Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

**Current Assets:** Cash, Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Prepaid Expenses
**Non-current Assets:** PP&E (net of depreciation), Goodwill, Intangible Assets, Long-term Investments

**Current Liabilities:** Accounts Payable, Accrued Expenses, Short-term Debt, Current Portion of Long-term Debt, Deferred Revenue (current)
**Non-current Liabilities:** Long-term Debt, Deferred Tax Liabilities, Pension Obligations

**Shareholders' Equity:** Common Stock, Additional Paid-in Capital, Retained Earnings, Treasury Stock (contra), Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income

## Cash Flow Statement

The Cash Flow Statement tracks actual cash movements over a period.

**Three sections:**

1. **Cash from Operations (CFO):** Start with Net Income, add back non-cash charges (D&A, SBC), adjust for changes in working capital.

2. **Cash from Investing (CFI):** CapEx (negative), acquisitions (negative), asset dispositions (positive), investment purchases/sales.

3. **Cash from Financing (CFF):** Debt issuance (positive), debt repayment (negative), equity issuance (positive), share buybacks (negative), dividends (negative).

**Ending Cash = Beginning Cash + CFO + CFI + CFF**

## Working Capital

**Net Working Capital (NWC)** = Current Assets (excluding Cash) - Current Liabilities (excluding Debt)

In practice, operational NWC = Accounts Receivable + Inventory - Accounts Payable

**Impact on cash flow:** An increase in NWC is a use of cash (negative on cash flow statement). A decrease in NWC is a source of cash.

**Example:** If Accounts Receivable increases by $10M, the company earned revenue but hasn't collected cash yet — that's $10M less cash despite the revenue being on the Income Statement.

## Depreciation and Amortization

**Depreciation:** Allocation of a tangible asset's cost over its useful life. Methods include straight-line (most common), declining balance, and units of production.

**Amortization:** Same concept applied to intangible assets with finite lives (patents, licenses).

**Straight-line formula:** Annual Depreciation = (Cost - Salvage Value) / Useful Life

**Key interview insight:** D&A is a non-cash expense. It reduces taxable income (creating a tax shield) but does not actually consume cash. That's why it's added back on the Cash Flow Statement.

**Tax shield from D&A = D&A × Tax Rate**

## Revenue Recognition (ASC 606)

Revenue is recognized when control of goods or services transfers to the customer, not necessarily when cash is received.

**Five-step model (ASC 606):**
1. Identify the contract with the customer
2. Identify the performance obligations
3. Determine the transaction price
4. Allocate the transaction price to performance obligations
5. Recognize revenue when (or as) performance obligations are satisfied

**Point in time vs. Over time:** Product sales are typically recognized at a point in time (delivery). Long-term service contracts are recognized over time (as services are rendered).

## Deferred Revenue and Deferred Expenses

**Deferred Revenue (Unearned Revenue):** Cash received before service is delivered. It's a liability on the Balance Sheet. Example: Annual software subscription paid upfront — revenue is recognized monthly as the service is provided.

**Prepaid Expenses (Deferred Expenses):** Cash paid before the expense is incurred. It's an asset on the Balance Sheet. Example: Prepaid rent — recognized as an expense each month.

## Key Financial Ratios

**Profitability:** ROE = Net Income / Equity; ROA = Net Income / Assets; ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital

**Liquidity:** Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities; Quick Ratio = (Cash + AR) / Current Liabilities

**Leverage:** Debt/Equity; Debt/EBITDA; Interest Coverage = EBIT / Interest Expense

**Efficiency:** Asset Turnover = Revenue / Assets; Inventory Turnover = COGS / Average Inventory; Days Sales Outstanding = (AR / Revenue) × 365
